Pokedeckz

Pokedeckz is an application for Pokémon Trading Card Game collectors to track their individual collections and interact with other hobbyists.

Project Overview

Vision Statement

Pokedeckz is a dedicated digital space for fans of the Pokémon Trading Card Game (TCG), designed to facilitate personal collection organization and global community engagement. The platform serves as a central hub where collectors can manage their inventories while connecting with a broader network of collectors and enthusiasts to discuss the hobby. By providing a unified platform for both management and social discovery, Pokedeckz allows fans to transform their physical binders and passion into a fun, shared digital experience.

The current iteration of Pokedeckz primarily functions as a detailed collection manager. Collectors can document their cards in great detail, recording professional card quality grades, market valuations, and personal notes for every item in their possession. Rather than laboring over manual data entry, hobbyists navigate a comprehensive digital catalog to update their status with ease. Pokedeckz also provides immediate access to vital card intelligence, from rarity and illustrator credits to competitive gameplay data like stage weaknesses and attack sets.

Extending beyond the individual, users are currently able to browse the libraries of fellow collectors and gauge their standing on community leaderboards, fostering a sense of friendly competition and discovery. This serves as the start of our application's social capabilities.

In the future, Pokedeckz will move towards a fully realized social ecosystem. Future iterations will introduce direct and group messaging, allowing hobbyists to chat in real-time to discuss their collections and strategies. The platform would also facilitate a peer-to-peer trading environment and an auction system, removing the logistical hurdles often associated with the exchanging of cards, while maintaining flexibility. These planned features will leverage the application's sophisticated tracking capabilities into an active, friendly marketplace for the global Pokémon community.

This platform represents a significant improvement over existing practices for participating in the hobby, which often rely on tracking collections on paper and in-person interactions at conventions. Centralizing the collection process saves significantly on travel costs and reduces the environmental impact associated with traveling to physical events. Pokedeckz also makes it much easier to track trades and card history, replacing the often unreliable, decentralized, individual tracking found at conventions. By providing a unified platform, the platform helps users establish trust through verified profiles and can help ensure the legitimacy of the cards being discussed and traded.

The project's success will be determined by examining the following three criteria. First, the platform will be evaluated by asking users to compare their experience with the platform against the traditional process of attending physical Pokémon trading card conventions. Second, the project must demonstrate statistically significant savings in terms of both time and money for collectors. Third, engagement with the hobby, over a year, will be compared between users of Pokedeckz and non-users. The project will be considered successful if all three criteria show an improvement over existing practices.

Intended Users

Our project's intended users include all kinds of Pokémon fans, from casual newbies and TCG players to superfans and sellers. It is especially built for collectors who need a way to easily track their cards in detail, like professional grades and market values. We also made this for people who want to share their collections and stay connected to the community at-large with the news feed. The different search and view options allow TCG players to check specific attacks and abilities while giving buyers and sellers quick access to the latest market trends.

Development Postmortem

Improvement Area from Iteration 2 Retrospective

One of the most important improvement areas we identified after our Iteration 2 retrospective was communication within the team. Earlier in the project our communication was not very structured. People would sometimes make significant changes without the rest of the team fully knowing about them, which occasionally caused problems when trying to integrate work between branches.

To improve this, we introduced two mandatory in-person meetings each week, usually on Mondays and Wednesdays. Before each meeting we prepared an agenda so that everyone knew what needed to be discussed and what progress we expected to make. We also created internal deadlines for features and asked everyone to post quick updates in our Discord channel whenever they made a major change or ran into an issue.

This improvement was successful. Communication within the team increased significantly and everyone had a much clearer understanding of what others were working on. The number of update messages in our communication channel increased substantially compared to earlier iterations. Because we stayed in constant contact and set internal deadlines, we also avoided the last-minute rush that had happened earlier in the project. Instead of scrambling to finish features right before the deadline, we were often able to spend the final few days of an iteration focusing on refactoring and reviewing code.

Overall Architecture of the System

As the project progressed, the architecture of the system improved significantly. Early on we had several design problems, including layering violations. For example, persistence related constants sometimes appeared in multiple layers, and certain logic such as sorting ended up in utility classes instead of more appropriate parts of the system.

We also initially had some very large classes that handled too many responsibilities, particularly in some activity and model classes. These classes were harder to maintain and made debugging more difficult. Over time we refactored many of these areas and distributed responsibilities more appropriately across the system. This helped make the code easier to understand and maintain.

Time Spent on Various Tasks

Another lesson we learned was how much time certain features require to fully implement. Features that seemed simple at first often required more work once testing, integration, and edge cases were considered. Each new feature also increased the complexity of the application and introduced more potential for technical debt.

Despite this, implementing a larger set of features made the project more engaging for our group and kept everyone motivated to contribute.

Outstanding Bugs or Technical Debt

The main area where technical debt still exists is in the presentation layer. Because the application contains many different pages and fragments, there is some duplication in UI elements such as toolbars and layout structures. Maintaining consistent layouts across multiple pages proved more difficult than we expected.

If development continued, one of the next improvements would likely be refactoring the UI structure so that these elements could be reused more easily and maintained in a more centralized way.

What Went Wrong

One of the biggest problems we encountered happened during Iteration 2 with our branching strategy. At one point we had many branches open that had not yet been merged into the main branch. These branches contained a large number of changes, and because they stayed open for so long, merging them became very difficult.

When we finally attempted to merge everything into main close to the iteration deadline, we encountered many merge conflicts. Even after resolving those conflicts, the main branch still contained several bugs and incomplete features because the changes from different branches did not integrate cleanly. Since this happened late in the iteration, our team had to scramble to stabilize the application and fix issues before the deadline.

To address this problem in Iteration 3, we changed our workflow. We began creating smaller branches and merged them into main much more frequently. This prevented branches from diverging too much and allowed us to catch integration issues earlier. As a result, Iteration 3 went much more smoothly and we were able to finish our work earlier without the last minute rush to fix bugs.

What Went Well

One thing that worked very well throughout the project was our regular team meetings and discussions. Every iteration we met frequently to talk about the current state of the project and reflect on mistakes we had made. These discussions were open and honest, and they helped create a positive and collaborative team environment.

More importantly, these meetings helped us avoid repeating the same mistakes. For example, during Iteration 1 we realized that we had not planned our features carefully enough and our workflow did not really follow an agile process. During Iteration 2 we improved our planning so that team members could work on different features at the same time. Then in Iteration 3 we improved again by merging branches more frequently to avoid integration issues.

Another strength of our group was how we divided work based on individual strengths. Some members focused more on architecture and backend functionality, while others contributed heavily to testing, UI development, or documentation. This helped the team work efficiently and allowed us to implement more features than we originally planned.

What We Would Change if Starting Over

If we were starting the project again, one thing we would likely do differently is spend more time researching architecture patterns used in similar applications before beginning development. Many of the improvements we made later in the project came from lessons we learned while working on it.

Having a better understanding of best practices from the beginning might have helped us avoid some of the early design problems and reduced the amount of refactoring we needed later.

Lessons Learned

Overall, the project evolved significantly from our original vision in Iteration 0. Early in development we focused mainly on getting the core functionality working. As the project progressed, we spent more time improving architecture, refining our workflow, and maintaining code quality.

Through this process we learned that communication, frequent integration of code, and regular reflection are essential when working on a large collaborative project. Even though we made several mistakes during the early stages, those experiences helped us grow as developers and ultimately led to a stronger final result.
